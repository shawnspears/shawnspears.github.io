Phase 0: Week 8

"Dude, should I max out my RAM?"

November 23rd, 2015

So for some reason now that I'm studying web development, my non-tech friends have taken this as an invitation to ask me questions regarding the computer hardware. "So should I go with 8GB or should I max it out?"

"Well I'm not exactly an expert on these things, but it really depends on what you normal use your computer for?"

I personally think that's a valid answer. If you're literally just a casual internet browser, it would be silly to max out your setup to that of, let's say a hardcore gamer who always chooses the highest visual setting in order to watch the sweat roll of of their OC. But I digress.

How DOES this memory thing even work? I think a good place to start would be from ground 0: bits. I'm sure you've heard of the binary system, which lays the foundation for how information is stored within a computer. All of the information stored in your computer is made up of 0's and 1's. That is it. We're back in 1999 you guys, because this is The Matrix. And intuitively that sort of makes sense because you cannot reduce it further than that. If a more real world example is more your thing, you can think of them as light switches, where 0 represents an off-state and 1 represents an on-state. Think of the ways in which everything you've interacted with on a computer can literally reduce down to 0's and 1's (images, video, text, facebook). A bit refers to a binary digit (0 or 1). Computers were actually made by wizards.

8 bits makes up 1 byte. Bytes are something we're definitely more famililar with. 1 billion bytes, makes up a 1GB: all of a sudden you realize that you could potentially store 32 billion 0/1's in your pocket. The future is now you guys.

You can even think of computer hardware such as your CPU, RAM, or HDD as being made up of the same sort of concept, as they can be broken down into tiny pieces of hardware called transistors that are essentially switches with two states of being: on or off. With advancements in computer engineering, these pieces or hardware are able to process more bits, and thus become more efficient!

Let's now go through how information flows through a computer. All bits must flow through the CPU (Computer Processing Unit), but it doesn't have a lot of memory (maybe 1MB), and only works on around 64 bits at a time. Thus, your files are fed in portions, and thus memory must be stored somewhere else temporarily and this is where RAM (Random Access Memory) comes in. RAM is where files and programs land when they are opened, as the bits are copied from the hard disk drive and stored temporily in RAM. RAM is much faster than your HDD, and yet your HDD contains more memory: this makes sense because you're not running all of the programs you have in your applications folder at the same time -- this is basically impossible because typically the standard amount of RAM in your laptop is around 4 to 8GB. Most of us run multiple programs at once, but the computer gives the illusion that this is happening. Innstead, the bits are fed to the CPU one program at a time, but because it is happening so quickly, it is impossible for us slow humans to notice.

So in general, the bigger the HDD you have, the more programs you can store. The more RAM you have, the more programs you can run at once. The faster your CPU, the faster the portions of bits get processed.

Again, I don't consider myself an expert on computer hardware at all, but hopefully this gives some insight on how information gets processed, and how it affects computer memory.
